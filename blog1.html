Why I Chose B.Tech CSE and Why KIET.

 June 16, 2026  ·   6 min read  ·   Personal Journey 

Choosing a career path is one of the earliest and most important decisions we make in life. Like many students, I also had to choose between several possibilities. But for me, the answer had always been hidden somewhere in my childhood.

1. Why I Chose B.Tech CSE

Computers have fascinated me for as long as I can remember. From a young age, I was curious about how software worked, how websites stored data, and how technology could solve real-world problems.

My first exposure to programming came in Class 6, when I tried learning Python. It was an exciting experience—I discovered that computers could be instructed to perform tasks through code. Unfortunately, due to limited resources and guidance, I couldn't continue exploring programming at that time.

Things changed in Class 8 when C++ was introduced in school. For the first time, programming became more than just curiosity—it became something I genuinely enjoyed learning.

But the real turning point came in Class 9, when our curriculum switched to Java.

Java changed the way I looked at programming. Object-oriented programming, GUI applications, and the ability to build complete software fascinated me. Even today, Java remains one of my favorite programming languages.

As I grew older, my interests expanded toward web development, data storage systems, and modern technologies like Large Language Models (LLMs). Every new concept made me more curious about computer science.

When it came time to choose an engineering branch, I never seriously considered anything other than Computer Science Engineering. CSE offers a vast landscape of opportunities-from software development and AI to entrepreneurship and research.

For me, choosing CSE wasn't just a career decision-it was a natural continuation of my curiosity.

2. Why KIET

When selecting a college, I wanted a place that balanced academic quality, opportunities, and accessibility.

KIET stood out for several reasons.

It has a strong placement record, a well-regarded Computer Science program, and its CSE department has earned NAAC A+ accreditation. Additionally, its proximity to my home was an important factor.

Another aspect that interested me was KIET's transition into a university. The institution has been updating its curriculum to align with industry demands, introducing skills that are increasingly relevant in today's rapidly evolving technology landscape.

While no college can guarantee success, I believe the right environment can provide opportunities—and it is ultimately up to students to make the most of them.

3. Expectations vs Reality

Before entering engineering, I imagined college life to be a perfect blend of academics, coding, innovation, and personal growth.

Reality turned out to be different.

I realized that college is not a place where knowledge is simply delivered to you. Instead, it provides an environment—and what you gain from that environment depends largely on your own initiative.

Some of the most valuable lessons I have learned did not come from classrooms but from building projects, debugging errors at midnight, reading documentation, and experimenting with new technologies.

Engineering taught me an important lesson: growth is self-driven.

4. What I've Learned

My learning journey began even before college officially started.

I had already learned HTML, CSS, and JavaScript and built several projects on my own. Through these projects, I discovered that programming is not just about writing code-it's about solving problems and creating experiences.

Web development taught me patience.

Debugging taught me persistence.

Projects taught me creativity.

And perhaps most importantly, technology taught me that learning never truly ends.

Every project I build today carries a lesson that no textbook could have taught me.

5. Advice to My Younger Self

If I could speak to my 16-year-old self, I would tell him:

"Work hard, but don't forget to live."

For a long time, I was completely focused on competitive exams. In that pursuit, I sometimes ignored my happiness, health, and well-being.

When the results did not match my expectations, I felt deeply disappointed. There was a phase when I lost motivation and struggled to restart.

Looking back, I realize that one difficult day—or even one difficult year—does not define an entire life.

Interestingly, despite not preparing as much as I could have after JEE Main, I still performed reasonably well in JEE Advanced. That experience taught me something important:

We are often far more capable than we think.

Success is important, but resilience is even more important.

6. Final Thoughts

Today, I see myself as someone who is still learning, still building, and still exploring.

My goal is to become a skilled full-stack developer and, one day, build products as an entrepreneur.

Technology evolves rapidly, and there is always something new to learn. That uncertainty excites me rather than scares me.

This journey—from a curious child experimenting with Python to a Computer Science student building projects—is only the beginning.

The road ahead is long, but I'm looking forward to every step of it.
